# Sleep Tactics — Research & Product Decisions

## Ranking methodology

Each tactic is scored on two dimensions:

- **Impact** — evidence-based effectiveness from sleep research (high=3, medium=2, low=1)
- **Doability** — real-world adherence; what people actually maintain in daily life, not just intend to try (high=3, medium=2, low=1)

**Rank = impact + doability, ties broken by doability.**

The rationale: a highly effective tactic that nobody sticks with is less useful to recommend than a moderately effective one with high adherence. Doability breaks ties because the best tactic is the one you actually do.

---

## Tactics

### 1. Cold room
**Impact: high · Doability: high**

Sleep onset requires core body temperature to drop 1–2°F. A cool room (65–68°F) accelerates this, deepening sleep.

Ranks #1 because it is both high-impact and passive — set the thermostat once, benefit every night.

*Sources: Matthew Walker "Why We Sleep", thermoregulation research*

---

### 2. Blue blockers
**Impact: medium · Doability: high**

Blue light delays melatonin release, pushing back your natural sleep window. Blocking it 2–3 hours before bed lets melatonin rise on schedule, so you fall asleep faster and at your intended bedtime.

Impact rated medium (not high) because the effect size is real but moderate — blue blockers help, but they don't overcome poor sleep hygiene on their own. Doability is high: once you own a pair, you just put them on.

*Sources: Huberman Lab, melatonin suppression research*

---

### 3. Mouth tape
**Impact: medium · Doability: high**

Mouth breathing causes more snoring and micro-awakenings through the night. Nasal breathing reduces these interruptions, giving you more continuous sleep and more time in deep sleep.

Impact rated medium because evidence is growing but less robust than lifestyle interventions. Doability is high: passive once you get past the initial weirdness, which typically goes away after 2–3 nights.

*Sources: Growing clinical evidence, Huberman Lab*

---

### 4. Caffeine cutoff
**Impact: high · Doability: medium**

Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors for up to 10 hours, reducing deep sleep even when you fall asleep without trouble. Cutting off by early afternoon (typically 1–2pm) removes this interference.

Doability rated medium because afternoon coffee is deeply habitual for most people. Achievable, but requires consistent intention.

*Sources: Walker "Why We Sleep", multiple RCTs*

---

### 5. Morning sunlight
**Impact: high · Doability: medium**

Triggers a morning cortisol pulse that anchors your circadian clock, making it easier to fall asleep at a consistent time at night. 10 minutes of outdoor light within an hour of waking is sufficient.

Doability rated medium because it requires going outside in the morning, which conflicts with indoor work routines and is weather-dependent.

*Sources: Huberman Lab, circadian biology research*

---

### 6.5. 528 Hz playlist
**Impact: low · Doability: high**

A calming playlist before or during sleep can reduce pre-sleep anxiety and establish a consistent wind-down ritual, both of which support faster sleep onset. Any benefit comes from the relaxation response — the 528 Hz frequency claim (a "solfeggio frequency" said to resonate with DNA or promote healing) is not supported by sleep research or peer-reviewed evidence.

Doability is high: passive, zero friction, just press play. Impact is low because the mechanism is general relaxation, not a targeted sleep intervention.

*Sources: General relaxation and sleep onset research; 528 Hz frequency claims are not evidence-based*

---

### 6. Consistent wake time
**Impact: high · Doability: low**

Anchors your circadian rhythm. Even one lie-in can delay your sleep phase by 1–2 hours, making it harder to fall asleep the following night. This is a core intervention in CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia), the gold standard clinical treatment for insomnia.

Doability rated low because it requires discipline every day including weekends. High failure rate in practice despite being one of the most evidence-backed interventions.

*Sources: CBT-I (core intervention), American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM)*

---

### 7. No alcohol
**Impact: high · Doability: low**

Alcohol sedates rather than induces sleep — it fragments the second half of the night and suppresses REM by up to 25%. The effect is present even at moderate consumption.

Doability rated low because alcohol is deeply social and habitual. Hard to maintain consistently across social contexts.

*Sources: Walker, polysomnography research*

---

## Notes on this list

- This list is intentionally short. Seven well-chosen tactics is more useful than an exhaustive catalogue.
- The ordering reflects what a first-time user should try first — start with passive, low-friction changes before tackling harder lifestyle ones.
- Custom tactics added by the user are not ranked and appear after this list in the Log screen.
